Grantsville Citizens Protecting Property Rights — Community Update

The City's claim over private irrigation shares is an overreach impacting residents today — and potentially everyone tomorrow.

What’s happening

Grantsville City is asserting ownership of private irrigation shares and requiring “shares or fees in lieu” for a service it does not provide. About 1,700 households have already been notified, with many more likely affected if this continues.

Key points

  • City is claiming ownership of private irrigation shares historically held by residents.
  • Demanding “shares or fees in lieu.” Under Utah’s Impact Fees Act, a charge imposed as a condition of approval is an impact fee even if called something else. See Utah Code §11-36a-204 (Other names for impact fees); also §11-36a-202 (Prohibitions on impact fees).
  • City ordinance update: The newly adopted ordinance states the City may hold transferred water rights or shares “for future use on the property or within the City.” Read the ordinance: Grantsville Ord. 2024-11 (PDF).
    “…transfer to the City of water rights or shares to be held by the City for future use on the property or within the City.”

  • Erodes property rights and property value.
  • Sets a precedent for further government overreach citywide.
  • City uses taxpayer resources; residents must self-fund their defense.

Updates / News

💧 What the City is doing With Our Water Rights

When we bought our homes, we were told that our irrigation water — was included with our property. Those irrigation shares were real, tangible property rights recognized under Utah law. They were appurtenant, meaning they legally belonged to each lot, just like your driveway or backyard.

But here’s what most of us didn’t know: Grantsville City forced developers to hand over those irrigation water shares “in lieu of” a fee for a service the City doesn’t actually provide. Developers complied, and the cost of those shares was rolled into the price of every home sold. So we paid for them — but the City ended up owning and controlling them.

⚙️ How the City Bundled Our Water Shares

Each subdivision’s dedicated irrigation water shares were documented at the state level under “water right numbers.” Think of a water right number — like 15-408 — as a tracking number for the combined irrigation shares tied to a group of homes or lots.

In the case of Heritage Grove, my development, developers were required to dedicate irrigation shares as a condition of approval. Those individual lot shares — including the ones attached to our homes — were bundled together by the City into a single recorded state water right, No. 15-408, which now sits under Grantsville City’s name at the Utah Division of Water Rights.

In other words:

The individual irrigation rights that once belonged to each homeowner are now grouped and controlled under a City-held file number — 15-408.

🧩 What Happened Next — NS058

In October 2023, Grantsville City filed an application with the State called NS058 (“Notice of Sewage Effluent Reuse”). That filing sought permission for the City to reuse treated wastewater — and to do it, they listed more than 60 water rights as their source water. One of those rights was 15-408 — the very bundle of irrigation water shares originally attached to many of our properties.

By listing it, the City treated our homeowner-funded irrigation water as City property — an asset to expand its own water supply for city-wide benefit.

🚨 Why That’s a Problem

City officials have told residents that the City would never use them for its own benefit. Yet the NS058 filing proves they already tried.

It shows that the City not only took control of homeowner-funded irrigation water, but then tried to repurpose it to increase their own water portfolio for city-wide reuse. That’s the definition of “double-dipping” — taking something we paid for, calling it City property, and using it to their advantage.

⚖️ The Outcome

The State ultimately denied the NS058 application after it was protested by the Friends of the Great Salt Lake for technical and environmental reasons. But the denial doesn’t erase what the filing exposed — that the City already claimed our subdivision’s irrigation rights as their own.

🧱 Plain Truth

Grantsville City required developers to give them irrigation shares for a service they don’t provide. We paid for those shares in our home prices. Then, behind closed doors, the City bundled those shares into large, City-held rights like 15-408 and tried to use them in a state-level filing to benefit its own operations.

That’s not an accident — that’s a system. And when the City claims it would “never use those shares for City benefit,” while the state’s own records say otherwise, that’s not honesty — that’s an insult to every homeowner’s intelligence.

✅ What We’re Doing About It

We’re fighting to expose this practice, stop it from continuing, and make sure the water rights we paid for remain where they belong — with the people who own the homes, not the City that took the benefit.
